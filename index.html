Content curation algorithms hide truth from the user in the name of personal relevance.

We built an interactive newsfeed simulator to show new social media users how these echo chambers are made.


Motivation

where does our simulator fit into the fake news cycle?

  • News on Social Media

    Citizens consume a large portion of their news via algorithmically-curated social media feeds.

  • An Echo Chamber is Born

    Algorithms track user interaction and show content similar to that which they've clicked in the past, amplifying a user's own initial biases and reflecting the biases of their peer groups.

  • Fake and Misleading Posts

    Satirical and outright false news stories enter the system for the profit and/or amusement of their authors.

  • You Don't Know What You Don't Know

    Users lack exposure to a broad sampling of contextualized news, making it difficult to distinguish fact from fiction. They see and share articles which reflect and reinforce their worldview, even if they may be false.

Overview


Problem

As our class explored the rise of fake news, our team focused on online echo chambers. Our research showed that most social media users didn't know why they were exposed to certain content or how their social media interactions could be affecting their worldview.

Solution

We decided to build a newsfeed simulator. We wanted to mimic the way social media sites curate content based on user interactions, but do so transparently so the user could obeserve exactly how their online behavior affects their exposure to certain types of posts.

Approach

Our team of three had four weeks to learn as much as we could about the problem space, refine our goal, and prototype a solution. We used a variety of system modeling and design techniques in order to do so, and our process is outlined below.

Research and Modeling


We conducted secondary research using published works in order to gain a broader academic understanding of the fake news ecosystem. Based on our findings, we wrote a page-long problem statement outlining the context for our work.

Several key findings stood out to us at this point. We noted the tension in how 'value-neutral' algorithms can cause systematically negative phenomena, and discussed the role of sensationalism in user engagement and content curation. At this point, we were interested in exploring the concept of satire, and how satirical news without proper context can be interpreted as truth and can thus function as both a detector of uncritical echo chambers, and an unwitting reinforcer of the same.

Finally, we combined our interview findings and secondary research findings to refine our system model. The above diagram is our resulting attempt to portray the smaller feedback loop of a piece of content going viral, within the larger feedback loop of social media, sensationalism, decontextualization, misinformation, mistrust, and socio-political toxicity.
